Master's Thesis Virtual Reality PTSD Treatment Adaptive Systems

Tailored Immersion: Adaptive Virtual Reality as a Tool for Personalized PTSD Treatment

Uppsala University — MSSc Human-Computer Interaction (Filosofie masterexamen) — 2024

VR exposure therapy for PTSD is well-established, but most implementations run the same sequence for everyone. This thesis looks at what a more personalised approach might look like, and what the design constraints would be.

The work combines a literature review with semi-structured interviews — one clinician specialising in trauma, two individuals with PTSD — and uses those findings to inform a proof-of-concept prototype. The prototype shows how traumatic elements could be introduced gradually, how the therapist could adjust the environment during a session, and what a clinician-facing interface might need to support that. A second psychiatrist reviewed the prototype and the design concepts behind it.

The result is a design direction rather than a tested intervention — user testing was out of scope — but it maps what adaptive VR for PTSD would need to account for, and where the tension between automation and therapist agency sits.

What the thesis did

  • Thematic analysis across expert and lived-experience interviews
  • Prototype demonstrating gradual, therapist-adjustable exposure
  • Therapist-facing interface concept for in-session control
  • Design considerations for clinically-grounded adaptive VR
  • Analysis of the tension between automation and therapist agency
Bachelor's Thesis Assistive Technology User-Centred Design

Increased Quality of Life for Physically Disabled Individuals: Factors to Consider When Developing Welfare Technology

University of Skövde — BSc Information Technology — Business Intelligence — 2022

Welfare technology for physically disabled users sits at the intersection of software engineering and human need — but the literature around how to actually develop it is fragmented. This thesis is a systematic literature review that asks which factors should be taken into account when building these products.

The review screened 71 articles from Google Scholar and Semantic Scholar against defined inclusion criteria, and analysed the final 15 through qualitative content analysis. Findings were organised into three categories: human factors, technical factors, and critical success factors.

The main result was a gap. The literature is rich on critical success factors and technical factors, but notably thin on human factors — which, given that welfare technology exists specifically to serve people whose needs are easily overlooked, is a problem. The consistent thread across all three categories was the importance of involving end users and stakeholders in development from the start.

What the thesis did

  • Systematic literature review across human, technical, and critical success factor literatures
  • Identified a gap in human-factor research within welfare technology development
  • Synthesised end-user and stakeholder involvement as a cross-cutting requirement
Group Project Embodied Interaction 2023

Bowling Time!

Read the full paper here (PDF)

A haptic glove that uses vibration patterns to help bowlers feel the correct release timing — built and tested at a real bowling alley.

This project explores bowling through the lens of embodied interaction, with focus on timing, rhythm, and consistency during the throw. Through observations and interviews with league players at Fyrishov Bowling Center, it became clear that timing is viewed as one of the most important aspects of the game. Players repeatedly described good throws as a harmony between body movement, steps, and the release of the ball.

The purpose of the project was therefore to investigate how technology could support the learning of this bodily knowledge, especially for newer players. Several concepts were developed and tested, including motion tracking and auditory feedback, before the final prototype became an interactive glove that delivers rhythmic haptic feedback while holding a real bowling ball. The vibrations were designed to guide the tempo of the swing and help players find a more stable release pattern.

The evaluations suggest that the concept had clear potential. Participants generally understood the intended purpose immediately and described the prototype as fun, useful, and easy to grasp. Multiple testers stated that it helped them notice when they were off pace. It was also argued that novice players would benefit the most, as they often need support in building repeatable movement patterns. During testing, one beginner player showed noticeable improvement across several throws, which was also observed by an experienced coach.

At the same time, some practical limitations were identified. The glove could feel restrictive or warm, and different playstyles required different adjustments. The conclusion of the paper is that subtle haptic feedback can be a meaningful way to support embodied skill development, particularly when the design works with natural movement rather than distracting from it.

Individual Assignment Measuring & Analysing UX 2023

Analysing Empowerment in Clue

Read the full paper here (PDF)

A critical walkthrough of the Clue app — looking at where the design supports user autonomy and where it quietly undermines it.

This paper examines the reproductive health application Clue, with focus on how its design features and functionality may support female empowerment regarding reproductive health. The purpose was to review if the application delivers the empowering experience it promotes.

Using the App Walkthrough Method together with a talk-aloud approach, the free version of the app was systematically reviewed. Features such as cycle tracking, calendars, symptom logging, analytics, and educational content were examined in relation to accessibility and user empowerment.

The findings show that Clue has clear empowering potential through insight, self-tracking, and informed decision-making. At the same time, subscription paywalls, extensive data collection, and advertisement-related privacy settings may limit that experience. The paper concludes that empowerment depends not only on useful features, but also on responsible design choices.

Literature Reflection Embodied Interaction 2023

E-Scooters, Materiality & Social Codes

Read the full paper here (PDF)

How the physical properties of e-scooters shape the way people ride them, where they ride them, and the social friction that follows.

This essay examines the use of e-scooters through the lens of embodied interaction, with focus on how material design and social codes shape the overall experience. The purpose was to explore how people interact with e-scooters physically, but also how expectations, norms, and surrounding environments influence that interaction. To support this, the Materials Experience framework was used together with first, second, and third-person perspectives.

The analysis suggests that the material properties of e-scooters have clear influence on user behaviour. Elements such as weight, balance, acceleration, braking, and standing posture all affect how the vehicle is experienced and navigated. It can be argued that these properties shape not only comfort and usability, but also how confident or responsible users behave in shared public spaces. Repeated interaction may also change attitudes over time, as users develop habits and tacit knowledge.

At the same time, the essay raises the issue of how e-scooters are perceived socially. Their quiet movement, speed, and presence on pedestrian routes may create uncertainty or frustration for others nearby. This means that the interaction is not limited to the rider alone, but extends into the surrounding social environment. The behaviour of one rider can therefore affect wider opinions regarding the technology itself.

The conclusion of the paper is that e-scooter use should be understood as both a material and social phenomenon. Suggested improvements included synthetic warning sounds, clearer infrastructure, and educational spaces where safer behaviour can be learned through experience. With more thoughtful design choices, it seems possible to support a more positive and responsible e-scooter culture.
