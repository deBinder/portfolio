Master's Thesis Virtual Reality PTSD Treatment Adaptive Systems

Tailored Immersion: Adaptive Virtual Reality as a Tool for Personalized PTSD Treatment

Uppsala University — MSSc Human-Computer Interaction (Filosofie masterexamen) — 2024

VR exposure therapy for PTSD is a well-established approach. The problem is that most implementations are static — same sequence, same pacing, regardless of how the patient in front of you is actually doing. This thesis looks at what an adaptive version would look like.

The proposed design is built around a 10-level gradual exposure system that adjusts based on behavioural and physiological signals, combined with a therapist-facing interface that gives clinicians real visibility into the session without interrupting it. A fixed protocol isn't fit for purpose here. The tool needs to respond to the person.

Key contributions

  • 10-level gradual exposure system with adaptive parameterisation
  • Therapist-facing control interface for real-time session management
  • Design framework for clinically-grounded adaptive VR
  • User-centred methodology with clinical stakeholders throughout
  • Analysis of the tension between automation and therapist agency
Bachelor's Thesis Assistive Technology User-Centred Design

Increased Quality of Life for Physically Disabled Individuals: Factors in Welfare Technology Development

University of Skövde — BSc Informatics — 2022

When you design assistive technology, the technical requirements are only part of it. Contextual, social, and user-specific factors tend to get deprioritised — or missed entirely. This thesis looks at what those factors are and why they matter.

The work maps where assistive tech development tends to fall short, and lays out a more grounded approach to it.

Key contributions

  • Analysis of key design factors in assistive technology development
  • Framework for user-centred AT design grounded in real-world context
  • Examination of where standard development approaches fall short for this population

Five-person group project. We went to Fyrishov Bowling Center to observe how people actually bowl — coaches, regular players, beginners — before touching any hardware.

The glove uses an Arduino and small vibration motors to cue the release moment. We made the tempo adjustable so it could match different playing styles. A bowling coach evaluated it with novice players and confirmed it produced real improvement.

What I found most interesting about it: timing is something that's normally taught by telling people to watch their hand, or by a coach standing next to them. We moved that cue into the body instead.

Used the App Walkthrough Method combined with talk-aloud to trace the gap between Clue's stated values and what the interface actually does.

Two things stood out: the app markets itself around user data ownership, but the business model includes ad-based tracking — that tension isn't visible anywhere in the UI. And core features are paywalled in ways that don't always map to what users actually need.

I also flagged that the original algorithm was developed without female researchers involved. That's not just a procedural issue — it affects what gets treated as a data point and what gets left out.

Applied the Materials Experience framework to look at how e-scooter design isn't neutral — the silence, the speed, the form factor all push toward specific behaviours, some of which create real problems in shared spaces.

The silence is a good example. It reads as clean and modern, but pedestrians can't hear scooters coming. That's not a user behaviour problem; it's baked into the material. I proposed a subtle synthetic engine sound as a possible intervention — enough to signal presence without being intrusive.

I also looked at designated learning areas as a way to let new riders build competence off shared pathways, shifting the onboarding problem away from "riders should just know better" toward something the infrastructure could actually support.
